A Beginner's Guide to AI Prompting

Learn a simple, effective approach to communicate with AI tools and create amazing resources for your classroom. No technical expertise required!

Getting Started with AI Prompting

Forget about complex prompt engineering guides. Instead, let's focus on a simple, practical approach that works for educators in real classroom situations.

When you're just beginning with AI tools, it's easy to get caught up in the "right way" to ask questions. You might worry about using perfect phrasing or finding the exact keywords.

But here's the good news: you don't need to overthink it. The most effective approach is much simpler and follows a natural conversational pattern. In this module, we'll introduce you to a straightforward framework that makes AI prompting accessible to everyone, regardless of technical background.

Step 1 of 5

A Simple Framework for Effective Prompting

Our approach breaks down effective AI prompting into three simple steps:

1. Express

Start by expressing your thoughts freely. Don't worry about spelling, grammar, or perfect organization. The AI is an expert in language and will understand you. It's about saving time, so just get your ideas out there.

Pro Tip: Write naturally as if chatting with a colleague. Typos and incomplete sentences are fineâ€”focus on your ideas, not perfect language.

2. Context

Include relevant details about your specific situation: year group, subject, specification points, student abilities, or learning objectives. Don't hesitate to upload photos of textbook pages, scans of notes, PDFs, or other resources to provide even richer context.

Pro Tip: Visual context like exam specifications, textbook screenshots, or past papers helps the AI understand exactly what you need.

3. Iterate

View prompting as a conversation, not a one-time query. If the first response isn't quite right, ask for revisions, clarifications, or a different approach. The AI improves with your feedback.

Pro Tip: Don't start over with a new prompt if you don't get what you want - build on what's already there.

See the Framework in Action

The best way to understand this approach is to see real examples of effective and ineffective prompts.

Primary: Year 3 Creative Writing Activity (English)

Less Effective Approach

"I need a writing activity about castles."

Why it's limited: No information about age group, skill levels, learning objectives, or time frame. The AI has no way to tailor the content appropriately.

More Effective Approach

"Im a yr 3 teacher at an indpendent school and need a creative writing activity about medieval castles. my class has mixed abilities with 3 very advanced writers and 4 who struggle with basic sentence structure. We've been learing about norman castles in history and ive attatched a photo of their history workbook pages for reference. I want a 45 min activity that includes a visual planning element and some vocabulary support."

Why it works: Despite spelling errors, it includes year group, school type, specific topic, differentiation needs, cross-curricular connections, visual reference, time constraints, and format requirements.

GCSE: Chemistry Revision Resource (Science)

Less Effective Approach

"Create a GCSE chemistry revision guide."

Why it's limited: No information about specific topics, exam board, student needs, or format. Too broad to be useful.

More Effective Approach

"I need a revision resource for my Year 11 GCSE Chemistry class (AQA exam board) on the topic of Organic Chemistry. Many students struggle with reaction mechanisms and naming compounds. I've attached the specification points they need to know. Could you create a one-page summary sheet with key reactions, common exam questions, and a visual mnemonic for the homologous series? My students respond well to color-coding and visual representations."

Why it works: Specifies year group, exam board, particular topic, specific student difficulties, includes specification points, requested format, and effective learning approaches for these students.

A-Level: Economics Essay Planning (Humanities)

Initial Prompt

"I teach A-level Economics (Edexcel) and I'm preparing for a 25-mark essay question on fiscal policy. The question is 'Evaluate the effectiveness of fiscal policy in reducing income inequality.' I've uploaded the specification and a screenshot of the mark scheme. Can you help me create a structured essay plan that would achieve top marks, including evaluation points and relevant examples my students can use?"

Follow-up Iterations

After the first response: "Thanks for this outline. Could you expand the section on progressive taxation with more recent UK examples? And my students often forget to include counter-arguments - can you strengthen that aspect?"

After the second response: "This is much better. My students are also studying monetary policy this term. Could you add a paragraph comparing the effectiveness of fiscal vs. monetary policy specifically for addressing inequality?"

Final refinement: "Perfect content now. One last thing - can you format this as a one-page handout with clear subheadings and perhaps a small diagram showing the circular flow of income?"

Why it works: Starts with rich context (exam board, question type, specific question, mark scheme) and then uses iterations to refine specific content needs, cross-topic connections, and final format.

FAQ & Resources

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to use special keywords or phrases?

No, you don't need special keywords or technical jargon. Modern AI tools understand natural language well. Just be clear and specific about what you need, as you would when explaining something to a colleague.

What if the AI gives me incorrect information?

AI systems can sometimes provide inaccurate information. Always review content critically, especially for factual subjects. If you spot an error, you can directly ask the AI to correct specific information. This is part of the iteration process.

How long should my prompts be?

There's no perfect length. Short prompts (1-2 sentences) work for simple requests, while more complex tasks benefit from longer, more detailed prompts (3-7 sentences). Include enough context for the AI to understand your specific situation and needs.

Can I use AI for curriculum planning?

Absolutely! AI can help generate lesson plans, curriculum maps, and long-term planning documents. Be sure to include details about your curriculum framework, assessment requirements, and school context. For best results, upload actual specification documents or syllabus materials directly to the AI. This provides the precise language and requirements needed for accurate planning. Remember to review and adapt the AI's suggestions to fit your specific teaching style and student needs.

What kinds of files can I upload to provide context?

Most modern AI tools allow you to upload a variety of files to provide richer context. These typically include PDFs, Word documents, images, PowerPoint presentations, Excel files, and text files. You can upload specification documents, textbook pages, student work samples (anonymized), lesson plan templates, or school policies to help the AI understand your specific context. The AI can analyze these files and use them as reference points when generating content for you.
